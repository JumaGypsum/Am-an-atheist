The intersection of atheism and politics.

Atheism, is often seen as a purely personal or philosophical stance. However, it also has political implications, both for atheists themselves and for the societies they live in. In this blog post, I will explore some of the ways that atheism and politics intersect, drawing on recent research and examples from different countries.

One of the most obvious ways that atheism and politics are related is through the atheist movement, which consists of various organizations, activists, and authors who promote atheism and secularism in the public sphere. The atheist movement emerged in the late 20th century, especially in Western countries, as a response to the perceived threat of religious fundamentalism and its influence on politics and culture. The atheist movement is often associated with the "New Atheism", a term coined by journalist Gary Wolf in 2006 to describe a group of outspoken and influential atheist writers, such as Richard Dawkins, Sam Harris, Christopher Hitchens, and Daniel Dennett. These authors have criticized religion as irrational, harmful, and incompatible with science and human rights.

The New Atheism has been described as a political project by some scholars, who argue that it has strong transformative goals, such as contesting the extensive role of religion in politics and promoting secularization, both in politics and society more generally. The New Atheists have advocated for the separation of church and state, the protection of free speech and expression, the promotion of scientific education and rationality, and the defense of human rights and equality for all people, regardless of their religious beliefs or lack thereof. The New Atheists have also challenged the idea that religion deserves respect or privilege in public discourse, and have argued that religious claims should be subjected to critical scrutiny and ridicule.

However, not all atheists agree with the New Atheist approach or agenda. Some atheists have criticized the New Atheists for being too aggressive, arrogant, or intolerant towards religious believers, or for being too simplistic or dogmatic in their views on religion and science. Some atheists have also questioned the political effectiveness or relevance of the New Atheist movement, especially in light of the increasing diversity and complexity of religious and non-religious identities and experiences in contemporary societies. Moreover, some atheists have expressed different or alternative political views or priorities than those of the New Atheists, such as socialism, feminism, environmentalism, or anti-racism.

Indeed, atheism does not necessarily entail a specific political orientation or ideology. Atheists hold a wide range of different political opinions and preferences, depending on their personal backgrounds, values, interests, and contexts. For example, some atheists may lean towards liberalism or social democracy, while others may favor conservatism or libertarianism. Some atheists may support progressive causes such as LGBTQ+ rights or racial justice, while others may oppose them. Some atheists may be politically active or engaged, while others may be apathetic or indifferent.

The political diversity of atheists reflects the fact that atheism is not a monolithic or homogeneous category, but rather a heterogeneous and dynamic one. Atheists differ not only in their political views, but also in their demographic characteristics (such as age, gender, race, ethnicity), their social identities (such as class, culture, nationality), their psychological traits (such as personality, values), their motivations (such as curiosity, rebellion), their experiences (such as discrimination), their affiliations (such as groups), their practices (such as rituals), their attitudes (such as certainty), their labels (such as agnostic), and their relationships (such as family).

The diversity of atheism also means that atheism is not equally distributed or represented across different countries or regions. The prevalence and visibility of atheism vary widely depending on various factors such as history, culture, politics, law, education, media etc. For instance, some countries have higher rates of atheism than others, such as China (where official statistics estimate that about half of the population is non-religious), Japan (where about 70% of people do not identify with any religion), France (where about 40% of people are either atheist or agnostic), or Sweden (where about 18% of people are atheist). In contrast, some countries have lower rates of atheism than others, such as Indonesia (where less than 1% of people are non-religious), Pakistan (where blasphemy laws make it dangerous to express disbelief in God), Nigeria (where about 90% of people are either Christian or Muslim), or Brazil (where about 8% of people are atheist).

The varying prevalence and visibility of atheism can have important political consequences for atheists in different contexts. In some countries, atheists may face legal or social discrimination, persecution, or violence, due to their lack of religious affiliation or their criticism of religion. For example, in Saudi Arabia, apostasy (leaving Islam) is punishable by death, while in Bangladesh, atheist bloggers have been hacked to death by Islamist militants. In other countries, atheists may enjoy legal protections or freedoms, but still face cultural or political challenges in terms of gaining social acceptance or political representation. For example, in the United States, atheists are still widely stigmatized and underrepresented in politics, despite their growing numbers and activism.

The intersection of atheism and politics, therefore, is a complex and multifaceted one, with both opportunities and challenges for atheists and non-atheists alike. Atheism can provide a critical perspective on the role of religion in politics and society, and can inspire social and political change in various ways. However, atheism can also be a source of conflict and division, especially when it is framed in a confrontational or exclusionary manner. To navigate the complexities of atheism and politics, it is important to recognize the diversity and complexity of atheism itself, and to engage in respectful and constructive dialogue with people of different beliefs and backgrounds.
